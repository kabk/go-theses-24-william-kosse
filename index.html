Metamorphic Memories:
Exploring the Interplay of technology
and Human Recollections

Graduation thesis
William Kosse
Royal Academy of Arts, The Hague, 2023-24
Graduation Supervisor Prof. Dr. Füsun Türetken

Abstract

This thesis in the relationship between memories and the transformative impact of artificial intelligence on human cognition. The research elaborates on the evolving landscape of memories and how AI technologies influence the formation, preservation and distortion of personal and collective memories.

Introduction

The dynamic, malleable nature of human memories, combined with their importance on our identity, emotional life and behaviour is dominated by an era of rapid technological progress. This thesis explores the complex relationship between technology and memory. Focusing on the themes of: transformative states of mind, cognitive disengagement, and the alienation experienced in the embrace of technological extensions. Human memory and technological influence blur boundaries.

Encouraged by a reflection on the interaction that shapes the narrative of our collective memories. The text goes through the nuances of how our recollections are shaped, preserved and occasionally distorted by the omnipresence of digital tools. Memory is explored in the face of extended cognition and active externalism, in relation to the rapid growth and prevalence of artificial intelligence.

Through the chapters, this symbiotic relationship is explored. Ranging from the nuanced connections between technology & memory to the psychological effects of technology-induced memory alterations. Memories, inter-minds, and the democratization of information is discussed. In contemplation of the future, the possibilities of Mechanically Extended Man and the intricacies of brain-computer interfaces are brought to light.

Nuanced connections between technology and memory

The boundaries of memory have expanded into a complex interplay between human cognition and the external world, raising questions on where the mind ends and the environment begins. The concept of extended cognition1 reveals that human reasoning is typically dependent on environmental supports. (Clark & Chalmers, 1998) The notion of active externalism2 is embraced, allowing us to go beyond a limited understanding of the mind and impact the way scientific investigation is approached and conducted. The implications extend beyond understanding the topic at its particular context; they reshape our understanding of memory's formation, retention, and recall.

The Internet has become the primary form of external or transactive memory3 in the age of information technology, which has altered the way information is stored and accessed collectively. In the study of the Google Effects on Memory, it is revealed that individuals, faced with knowledge gaps, instinctively turn to computers for information, treating them as external memory systems that can be accessed at any time. (Sparrow et al., 2011) This relationship with our digital tools has led to a transformation where individuals remember not just what they know but, where to find the information. (Sparrow et al., 2011)

The concept of transactive memory, as studied by Daniel M. Wegner, extends beyond individual cognition to the collective. When individuals share information within a group, a collective memory4 system forms, creating a shared knowledge pool. (Ward, 2013) For instance, in a collaborative research project, team members with distinct expertise, like data analysis, design, and data manipulation, contribute their specialized knowledge to the group. Each team member serves as a repository of expertise in their respective fields, sharing and offloading information related to their skills with others in the team. This concept questions the conventional perspective that regards human memory as the ultimate form of information storage, introducing the idea of treating the Internet as a transactive memory system. (Ward, 2013) The evolution of this partnership is so profound that “the experience of losing our Internet connection becomes more and more like losing a friend.” (Sparrow et al., 2011) This analogy gives light to the emotional depth of our digital interactions. Through these implications we are led to question how this change affects our cognitive processes and the essence of our identity.

This evolution comes with its complexities and consequences, according to the Google effect study, individuals who relied on information stored on a computer experienced a reduction in their ability to recall information. (Sparrow et al., 2011) This implies that, relying heavily on external memory systems, such as: computers, photos and maps, may lead to a diminished capacity for remembering information independently. The rise of the Inter-mind5 challenges the very essence of individual identity, cognitive ability, whilst making us vulnerable to manipulation and at a bigger risk to loss of personal connection. The exploration of memory's nuanced connections with technology extends beyond academia into popular culture. The 'Black Mirror' episode 'The Entire History of You' is a dystopian narrative where memory, surveillance, and control intersect. (‘Black Mirror’, 2024) The episode portrays a society where memories are no longer ephemeral but are recorded and replayed, blurring the lines between present experience and past recollection. The ability to remember everything can be a double-edged sword, allowing for negative memories to be suppressed but also posing concerns about continuous surveillance as a means of control. (Robot, 2023) This problem can be reflected in world concerns, notably in China, where the government implemented a social credit system to monitor and manage its citizens – the system influences creates a society where that conforms to an ulterior motive. “The Entire History of You" serves as a warning, highlighting the potential consequences of integrating technology into various facets of our lives.

Technological advancements not only enhance our memory by providing efficient tools for storing and retrieving information but also redefine how we remember through the outsourcing of memory functions to external systems like cloud storage. The concept of autobiographical memory takes centre stage. Technology enables us to selectively capture, document, and share specific moments, shaping our personal narratives. The digital era empowers individuals to curate and filter their autobiographical memories, influencing not only how we remember but also how we construct and present our life stories; societal and cultural shifts further normalise this phenomenon. This raises concerns about the balance between technological progression and the possible threat to psychological well-being. Hence, there is a pressing need to contemplate the dual nature of this advancement and examine the fundamental aspects of human existence within this interconnected relationship with technology.

[1] Extended cognition: Takes the idea that your mind is ‘on’ your smartphone literally. It says that human cognitive states and processes sometimes spill outside our heads and into objects in our environment.

[2] Active externalism: A perspective that suggests external features or elements play a significant role in shaping our daily lives.

[3] Transactive memory: A theory about how people in relationships, groups or teams (we use these two terms interchangeably), and organizations learn "who knows what" and use that knowledge to decide "who will do what," resulting in more efficient and effective individual and collective performance (Hollingshead et al., 2008)

[4] Collective memory: It refers to the memories that individuals have as members of the groups to which they belong, whether small (family, school) or large (political party, nation). (Roediger, 2021)

[5] Inter-mind: The term refers to the collaborative nature of memory, emphasizing how individuals share and rely on external sources, such as other people or technology, to collectively store and retrieve information. It highlights the social and distributed aspects of memory processes. (Sparrow et al., 2011)

Prompting Contemplation

In a world increasingly shaped by technology, the interaction between our memories and digital spaces encourages contemplation about the consequences of these connections. It is important to take into account the effects of personalised information, automation, artificial intelligence, and the evolution of our relationship with technology.

The personalised web and its impact on individual perspectives are revealed through Eli Pariser's book 'The Filter Bubble'. He argues that while the democratisation of information6 was previously celebrated for eradicating centralised power, now it is raising concerns about the homogenisation of viewpoints. (Pariser, 2011) In the field of online news consumption, the algorithmic curation of content threatens diversity, potentially isolating individuals within their own beliefs. (Dujeancourt & Garz, 2023) This suggests that within these bubbles7, there is a tendency for only one's own viewpoints to be mirrored, limiting exposure to diverse perspectives and ideas. As such, we are confronted with the paradox of information access — while we gain immediate access to tailored content, we risk narrowing our exposure to diverse opinions and ideas. In this an era of tailored content delivery, the prevalence of automation contributes to an environment where algorithms shape our experiences.

Brett Frischmann's TED (Frischmann, 2020) talk dives into the automation bias8 and unexamined techno-social engineering9 that permeates various aspects of our lives. From automated responses in social media interactions to the integration of technology in education, the pervasive influence of automation challenges us to reconsider the unintended consequences of our reliance on technology. (Frischmann, 2020) Our dependence on information retrieval may foster automation bias, reinforcing our existing belief and preferences whilst elevating the risk of narrowing our exposure to diverse opinions. This complex relationship highlights the possible impact on our critical thinking and decision-making abilities. The increasing tendency towards automation bias and limited exposure to diverse opinions interrogates on the prospective evolution in human cognition. The nature of human thought and experience is on the verge of substantial shift, with the potential emergence of limited perspectives and automated decision-making in a scenario where human consciousness is simulated in a digital realm, influenced by these prevalent patterns.

In the book ‘The Age of Em', Robin Hanson's explores of the 'em' era; it involves emulating human minds onto computers, creating a world where biology meets the digital, pushing us into a speculative future where humans undergo a transformation by being emulated onto computers. This concept is not limited to a technological feat; it marks a paradigm shift in our understanding of existence and consciousness. In this scenario, the essence of what it means to be human exceeds its traditional boundaries. The 'emulated onto computers' notion challenges preconceived notions about the constraints of our physical bodies and the potential for consciousness to extend beyond biological confines. This futuristic scenario forces us to confront the implications for identity, cognition, and our subjective reality. As such, questions arise about the nature of consciousness, the preservation of memory, and the ethical considerations surrounding the fusion of human essence with computational machinery. In essence, 'emulated onto computers' invites us to envision a future where the boundaries between the organic and the artificial blur into a new human experience. Our fundamental assumptions about morality, progress, and identity are challenged in an era where humans are no longer limited by biological constraints but can exist as digital entities. Perhaps Hanson’s speculative thoughts on a future, in which digital bodies take over the physical aspects of being a human, isn’t so far from our present reality.

Nicolas Carr's "The Glass Cage" offers a critical examination of automation's impact on various aspects of our lives. (Carr, 2015) Carr questions the hidden costs of convenience and efficiency when it comes to automating physical labour and delegating mental tasks. When we hand over tasks to machines, we run the risk of losing certain skills and diminishing our capacity for curiosity, imagination, and independence. (Carr, 2015) In that way, part of our physical functions is outsourced to technology, and we are one step closer to existing as Hanson’s digital emulations of human minds - these emulated minds, existing as digital entities, retain the cognitive abilities, memories, and consciousness of their human counterparts.

The essence of progress and the values we hold dear are being questioned through this chapter. Pariser, Frischmann, Hanson, and Carr's narratives are signs that prompt us to consider the trade-offs in our technologically infused lives. We are faced with the delicate balance between progress and conserving what makes us distinctly human.

Malleability of Human Experiences in the Digital Age

Tim Wu's book 'Attention Merchants' explores the relationship between technology and human consciousness in the attention economy10. From the early days of newspapers to the relentless bombardment of advertisements in the digital era (Wu, 2017), attention has become a commodity traded for profit.

The people who seek attention to influence behaviour, especially to drive purchases, are called Attention Merchants. This industry, emerging less than 200 years ago, traces its roots back to the first world war when propaganda campaigns marked the transition from forcefully taking the attention of the people to a more subtle approach. (Adeoti, 2020) In the early 1800s, Benjamin Day, a pioneer, introduced the concept of true advertisement and became one of the world's first attention merchants. The narrative unfolds through history, including the radio's role in Hitler's propaganda and the rise of computers & smartphones - platforms that penetrate an ever-growing portion of our waking lives (Tarnoff, 2016)

Wu's book poses a polemic: the attention industry poses a threat to human happiness by inhibiting good attention, which is deep, long-lasting, and voluntary, while encouraging bad attention, which is quick, superficial, and often involuntarily provoked. (Tarnoff, 2016) Digital life is described as corrupt11 and is highlighted by the influence of the attention industry. The pervasive use of attention-grabbing techniques, often at the expense of users' time and focus, reflects a distortion of genuine, meaningful engagement with digital content. The corruption of the digital attention industry is no longer news. Wu’s description of it is almost a decade old and, although its relevance has persisted, if not increased, new complexities of ‘corruption’ in our digital lives have entered the picture. In Wu’s example, the user is the victim of the corruption and manipulation. But what happens when the user becomes an active part of corrupting?

Image manipulation, previously a skill perfected by professional editors, is entering the average smartphone users’ toolbox. Marques Brownlee, a YouTuber with grand success in reviewing the newest consumer electronics, examines a new flagship smartphone (Marques Brownlee,2024) that uses Artificial Intelligence powered by Google Cloud. The YouTube video introduces us to the interconnectedness of hardware that harnesses the power of the Neural Processing Unit (NPU)12, revolutionises image processing capabilities. This new smartphone will have a transformative impact on mobile photography. (Adebanji, 2024) Changing the way individuals interact with and manipulate visual content, smartphone owners will not only be able to move, resize subjects, or completely remove them, it can also generate background and gaps one might have in their photos; the image manipulation offers a small opportunity to retouch parts of one’s own past. Thus, the ethical considerations surrounding AI in photography, from authenticity to representation and privacy, are serious. (Adebanji, 2024) The manipulation of memory brings up challenges with implications for both individual and collective narratives. The very nature of external algorithm created by corporation shape the way we recall and interpret our experiences. Beyond the immediate impact on personal recollections, there is a broader question about the elasticity of historical events in the digital age. As technology becomes an integral part of memory formation and preservation (Rational Expressions Inc., n.d.), AI-powered tools have the potential to alter memories by manipulating photographs and changing the visual representation of past events. Memory distortion13, a natural part of building a coherent sense of self, encounters a double-edged sword as AI-powered tools offer both creative potential and ethical dilemmas.

Users are taking on a more active role in manipulating thoughts and, particularly, memory through AI-generated imagery. Although it may seem like they have some control over creating and perfecting their past through these technologies, a deeper reflection reveals a more complex reality. Despite the appearance of empowerment, the user remains, in essence, a victim. The paradox begins with our desire to 'perfect' and reshape our past, a narrative that was skilfully sold to us by the attention industry. The attention economy, driven by the relentless pursuit of profit, subtly planted the seed of discontent, convincing individuals that the idealised versions of their past could be achieved through the very tools now at their disposal. The blurring boundaries between user agency and industry influence leads to users being both orchestrators and subjects within memory manipulation.

Psychological effects of technology induced memory alteration

The intersection between human cognition and the external world leads to a multifaceted exploration of cognitive, emotional, and identity-related dimensions when technology and memory interact. The psychological effects of technology on memory extend beyond storage and retrieval, encompassing a transformative state of mind14 within a broader narrative.

Nicholas Carr's "The Glass Cage: Automation and Us" dives into the repercussions of automation on human cognition. He warns of cognitive disengagement15 as technology assumes more cognitive tasks, leading to a state of mental passivity. (Carr, 2015) Carr argues that automation can narrow our perspectives, limit our choices, and, in turn, impact the depth and quality of our memories. The danger is not just about technology taking over cognitive tasks, but also about the decline of active, thoughtful engagement with the world.

A lens through which we examine the personal emotional facets of technology-induced memory alteration is Björk's song” All is Full of Love". (Björk, 2007) The music video shows how technology and our emotional experiences come together in a harmonious yet unsettling convergence. The machine feels as human as, the human feels machine. The symbiosis between these two "species" is intricate. Through moments of harmony or tension, our emotional experiences and technology become connected and interdependent, resembling the yin and yang of ancient Chinese philosophy (Bellaimey, 2013). Consequently, introducing the topic of alienation in the digital age. The song "OK Computer" by Radiohead serves as a sonic exploration of a dystopian future shaped by technology. The album navigates through a narrative that explores the impact of technology on societal expectations and individual identity. (‘OK Computer’, 2024) This exploration intersects with the construction of collective memories. Once again, the relationship to technology is portrayed as a harmonious collaboration, where the interplay of mutually beneficial aspects and contrasting forces shapes the dynamics between humans and technology.

In our interaction with technology, we exercise control and authority, and with control comes the ability to choose. Computing operates on a binary basis, where the options are reduced to 0 or 1, representing yes or no. When applied to the idea of memory, this signifies the choice between preservation and erasure. The film “Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind" plunges us into the heart of memory manipulation technology. (‘Love, Memory, and Identity’, 2023)> The films memory erasure procedure symbolises society's desire to escape emotional pain and unpleasant memories. Ultimately, the film prompts contemplation on the implications of selectively editing personal narratives. Acknowledging and integrating past experiences, even the painful ones, leads to growth. It raises the fundamental question: Can we truly be human without our memories? The Blade Runner saga, encompassing both its 1982 and 2017 iterations, extends this exploration by introducing artificial memories. In the original film, photographs are used because they are durable in their filmic structure much like memories—they serve as tangible proof that the past can be recollected with a degree of certainty. Capturing a moment through photography creates a feeling of reality; even if a photo fades, its existence remains. Blade Runner 2049, the sequel, is largely the story of a man sorting through pasts both real and manufactured to form a coherent identity. However, the nature of memory undergoes transformation in this sequel. The shift is attributed how memories are stored on the cloud, an online network of data storage. Just like the outsourced memories, such as remembering an event through an image, externalised data becomes susceptible to external influences, making it malleable, modifiable, and, at times, vulnerable to corruption. In that way, the process of memory corruption is comparable to a dropped vase —the memories may shatter into myriad pieces, but their presence persists. In the film Inception, 2010, the audience is plunged into a world where individuals can enter dreams and manipulate the subconscious, leading to an examination of memory, reality, and identity. (Sumner, 2017) Our understanding of selfhood is intricately tied to the memories we carry. Memories serve as the building blocks of personal identity. The manipulation of memories in the dream world directly impact the stability and coherence of personal identity. Authentic or manipulated, memories bear an emotional weight on one's being. When the line between reality and the subconscious are blurred, the psychological toll of navigating a world where memory itself becomes a construct is accentuated. In inception, the dream world becomes a canvas where the mind paints and erases, challenging conventional notions of a fixed reality. Thus, the essence of identity lies not solely in memories but rather in the interpretation of these memories; one's identity is intricately tied to the subjective lens through which memories are perceived and understood. When memories are subject to manipulation, the very core of identity is called into question.

[14] Transformative state of mind: State characterized by profound changes in perception, beliefs, or attitudes, often resulting from significant experiences or influences.

[15] Cognitive disengagement: A set of symptoms defined by excessive mind-wandering, mental confusion, and slowed behaviors.

Future Trends

In a future lead by humans in symbiosis with technology, several possibilities are at the forefront: the evolution of the Mechanically Extended Man16, (Licklider, 1960) a human-machine symbiosis and BCIs17 influencing the temporal dimensions18 of our memories.

A scope towards our immediate future leads us to the concept of the Mechanically Extended Man; envisioning a future where technology augments capacities, while respecting individual autonomy. Its presence is already evident in industries like machining, where operators oversee machines that cannot be fully automated. This application reflects the essence of respecting individual autonomy. As this concept continues to evolve, it remains user-centric, designed to enhance experiences without overshadowing human agency. This balanced evolution points to a future where technology empowers, coexisting harmoniously with nuanced human control.

Yet, the trends in new technologies points towards a seamless integration of wearables, ranging from smart watches to brain- computer interfaces, into our existence. The concept of human-machine symbiosis goes beyond conventional interactions - its integration is so smooth that technology becomes an extension of the user, influencing and enhancing various aspects of our cognitive functions and daily activities. Neurotechnological advancements enable direct interactions between minds and technology, this could allow for collaborative memory projects and interconnected ecosystems, for a collective consciousness enhancing the collective understanding of the subject. Imagine a scenario where individuals, equipped with BCIs (Emotiv, n.d.), can participate in collective memory initiatives, presenting a use case that surpasses the current capabilities of such devices. For instance, a research project focused on understanding historical events could involve participants sharing their recollections related to a particular period. Digitally stored and transferred memories will then make up a comprehensive and interconnected database. Thus enabling, a collective consciousness that enhances our collective understanding of various subjects.

However, due to these new possibilities, ethical considerations may emerge. Notably in the regard of individual identity. The interaction between temporal dynamics, human-machine integration and perception, is complex in nature. As computers operate quickly and memories evolve, we find ourselves simultaneously recalling and envisioning the past. This investigation encourages reflection on the ethical dimensions of temporal experiences. One key aspect is the ethical use of technology to influence the way individuals experience and understand time. This raises questions about privacy, consent, and the potential impact on personal making independent decisions and controlling our own actions without external interference. By allowing technologies, such as television, and applications, such as Instagram and YouTube, to access and manipulate temporal data the ethical dimensions extend to societal implications - the adoption of these technologies may lead to societal shifts in how people relate to time, altering cultural norms and values.

[16] Mechanically Extended Man: In the man-machine systems of the past, the human operator supplied the initiative, the direction, the integration, and the criterion. The mechanical parts of the systems were mere extensions, first of the human arm, then of the human eye. (Licklider, 1960)

[17] Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs): A device that allows users to voluntarily share specific memories or information directly from their minds.

[18] Temporal dimension (time dimension): Speaks of the influence of time on our memories and how our perception of events changes over time. It encompasses how memories are shaped, recalled, and interpreted in relation to the temporal aspects of when the events occurred.
